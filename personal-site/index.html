Core Beliefs & Values

Data as a Public Good

Information created by government belongs to the people. Open data isn't just transparency—it's the foundation of democratic accountability. I believe every city should publish machine-readable datasets by default, with rare exceptions for privacy and security.

Technology Must Serve Equity

Smart cities can't replicate the inequalities of the past. I work to ensure that digital infrastructure, algorithmic systems, and civic technology platforms are designed with marginalized communities at the center—not as an afterthought.

Participation Over Performance

Town halls and public comment periods often exclude working families who can't attend 6 PM meetings. Real civic engagement happens when we meet people where they are—through asynchronous digital platforms, multilingual outreach, and childcare-supported events.

Evidence-Based Pragmatism

I'm not an ideologue. I care about what works. Every policy I advocate for is backed by data, piloted in real communities, and evaluated rigorously. If the evidence changes, so should the policy.

Why This Work Matters to Me

I grew up in a neighborhood where city hall felt like a distant, inaccessible fortress. When my mom needed a business permit, she had to take time off work, navigate confusing forms, and wait in line for hours. When our street flooded every winter, we had no way to know if the city even knew about it—let alone had a plan to fix it.

That experience shaped everything I do. I believe government should be as easy to access as ordering groceries online. I believe data about our communities should be as available as weather forecasts. And I believe that technology—when designed thoughtfully—can close the gap between people and power.

My work is personal. Every dataset I help publish, every algorithmic audit I conduct, every civic platform I design is rooted in one question: Would this have helped my mom? Would this have helped my neighborhood? If the answer is no, we're building the wrong thing.

FEATURED ESSAY • The Atlantic • Jan 2026 The Transparency Paradox: Why Open Data Isn't Enough

Cities are publishing more data than ever. Yet citizens feel less informed than before. The problem isn't transparency—it's legibility. Raw datasets don't empower people; they overwhelm them. True democratic participation requires data that's not just open, but understandable, contextual, and actionable.

In this essay, I argue that the open data movement has succeeded technically but failed democratically. We've won the battle for transparency but lost the war for accountability. I propose a new framework: "Civic Legibility Standards"—a set of design principles that mandate not just data publication, but data comprehension.
